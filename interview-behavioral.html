INFLUENCE WITHOUT AUTHORITY

Q: Tell me about a time you had to influence without authority 90 sec

SITUATION We discovered that multiple teams were creating Azure Storage blobs for static websites without HTTPS ‚Äî data exposed to the public, unencrypted. My manager asked me to figure out a process to find and track these.

TASK His initial suggestion was to develop a Python or PowerShell script to scan and identify these misconfigurations. It was a reasonable approach, but I thought there might be a better way.

ACTION I agreed that scripting could work, but paused to think through the problem. I realized we already had diagnostic logs and Azure Activity logs enabled. Instead of building a separate scanning tool, we could write a KQL query to identify exposed storage accounts and track them in a Sentinel workbook.

I pitched this to my manager: "Instead of a script that runs periodically, we get real-time visibility. The workbook shows current state, tracks remediation progress, and we can set up alerts for new violations."

For the actual remediation ‚Äî converting to HTTPS and enabling encryption ‚Äî we did use PowerShell, but the execution and results were also tracked in the same workbook.

RESULT My manager accepted the approach as a better solution. We implemented the workbook, and now we have continuous visibility into storage security posture rather than point-in-time scans. The same pattern has been reused for other compliance checks.
Why this works: Shows you can respectfully push back on a manager's suggestion with a better approach
Key message: "I didn't just disagree ‚Äî I offered a solution that solved the underlying problem better"
Deep dive: Workbooks & Dashboards
CROSS-FUNCTIONAL COLLABORATION

Q: Tell me about a time you resolved a conflict between teams 60 sec

SITUATION During the MDE deployment, IT Ops had created a golden image with the MDE agent pre-installed. When they deployed it, 50+ devices ended up with duplicate device IDs ‚Äî they couldn't be uniquely identified in the console.

CONFLICT IT Ops wanted to re-image all affected machines. That would take weeks and disrupt users. I knew there was a better way ‚Äî offboard, wait for the cleanup period, re-onboard with fresh IDs. But I had no authority over their team and they were resistant to my suggestion.

ACTION Instead of arguing, I offered to prove it. I took 5 pilot machines, ran the offboarding script, let them sit for the required period, then re-onboarded. Each got a clean, unique device ID.

I documented the process, showed them the results, and offered to create the remediation script for the rest. I also helped them update the golden image SOP so it wouldn't happen again ‚Äî install MDE but don't onboard, onboarding happens at first boot.

RESULT They agreed to my approach. We fixed all 50 devices without re-imaging. More importantly, IT Ops started coming to me for advice on other MDE issues because I'd proven I was there to help, not criticize.
Why this works: Proved with evidence, didn't argue, made their life easier, built relationship
Key message: "I influenced by demonstrating, not by demanding"

3Ô∏è‚É£ Challenges & Failures

BIGGEST FAILURE

Q: Tell me about your biggest failure 60-90 sec

SITUATION During MDE rollout, I enabled an ASR rule that blocks unsigned PowerShell scripts. I tested it in audit mode for 2 weeks ‚Äî no alerts, looked clean. Felt confident we'd caught everything.

WHAT WENT WRONG Two days after enabling block mode, Finance escalated ‚Äî their month-end reporting scripts were being blocked. These scripts only run monthly, so my 2-week audit window completely missed them.

IMPACT Finance couldn't run their month-end reports. I had to create an emergency exclusion while we fixed it properly.

WHAT I LEARNED Audit windows need to cover full business cycles, not arbitrary time periods. Now I run audit mode for a full month minimum for any blocking rule, and I specifically ask stakeholders about scheduled tasks ‚Äî weekly, monthly, quarterly processes.

PROCESS IMPROVEMENT I created a pre-enforcement checklist that includes confirming with business units about periodic scripts. That checklist is now part of our standard ASR deployment process.
Key elements: Own the failure, explain what you learned, show systemic fix
Key message: "2-week audit wasn't enough ‚Äî I should have asked about monthly processes"
Deep dive: ASR Rules
DELIVERING BAD NEWS

Q: Tell me about a time you had to deliver bad news 60 sec

SITUATION At a previous role, we had FireEye HX with URL inspection enabled. Users at a subsidiary started reporting that their HR portal wasn't loading properly ‚Äî pages would hang, forms wouldn't submit.

INVESTIGATION After troubleshooting, I found that the HR portal used JavaScript components that FireEye's URL inspection was blocking. I opened a support case with FireEye ‚Äî they confirmed it was a known limitation with no fix available at that time.

THE BAD NEWS I had to tell leadership: "There's no fix. We have to choose between full security coverage or a working HR portal."

ACTION I didn't just bring the problem ‚Äî I brought options. Option A: Keep it broken (unacceptable). Option B: Create a separate device group for those 3-4 HR systems and apply a security profile with URL inspection turned off, while documenting the accepted risk.

RESULT Leadership accepted Option B with the documented risk. We created the separate group, monitored those devices more closely, and months later a vendor update fixed the issue and we re-enabled full inspection.
Key elements: Thorough investigation, brought options not just problems, documented risk
Key message: "I don't just deliver bad news ‚Äî I bring solutions"

4Ô∏è‚É£ Consulting-Specific Scenarios

CLIENT DISAGREEMENT

Q: How would you handle a client who disagrees with your recommendation? 60 sec

First, I'd make sure I understand why they disagree. Is it technical concerns? Budget? Politics? Timeline? The surface objection often isn't the real issue.

If it's a legitimate difference of opinion, I'd present the tradeoffs clearly. "Option A has these benefits and these risks. Option B has these. Here's my recommendation and here's why, but here's what you'd need to mitigate if you go the other way."

Ultimately, it's their organization. My job is to give them the best advice and the information to make a good decision. Document the recommendation, document their decision, move forward as a partner.

The one exception: if it's a security decision that creates unacceptable risk ‚Äî like disabling MFA to make logins faster ‚Äî I'd escalate within our firm before proceeding.
Shows: Client-centric thinking, professional judgment, knowing when to escalate
CLIENT ETHICS

Q: A client asks you to do something you think is wrong. What do you do? 60 sec

Depends on what "wrong" means.

If it's unethical or illegal ‚Äî like falsifying audit evidence ‚Äî that's a hard no. I'd push back clearly, and if they insist, escalate immediately to my engagement manager and our risk team.

If it's technically wrong ‚Äî a bad architecture decision ‚Äî I'd explain the risks clearly, document my recommendation, but ultimately respect that it's their decision. They might have context I don't have.

If it's out of scope ‚Äî "Can you also fix our network while you're here?" ‚Äî I'd clarify scope boundaries and discuss whether this should be a change order.

The key is distinguishing between "I disagree" and "this is a red line."
UNKNOWN PROBLEM

Q: What if you realize you don't know how to solve the problem? 45 sec

Consulting is a team sport. First, I'd leverage the firm's network ‚Äî who's done this before? What knowledge bases exist? What past engagements are relevant?

I'd be transparent with the engagement manager: "Here's what I know, here's where I need support." Better to ask early than struggle silently.

With the client, I'd manage expectations without undermining confidence. "Let me research this and get back to you by end of day" is better than guessing.

In my experience, the ability to quickly learn and find answers is more valuable than already knowing everything. That's how I approached the security transformation ‚Äî I hadn't done a full Microsoft stack migration before, but I knew how to figure it out.

GOING ABOVE AND BEYOND

Q: Tell me about a time you went above and beyond 60 sec

SITUATION At a previous role, we had FireEye HX deployed across the environment. Compliance checks were completely manual ‚Äî every week, someone had to log into multiple HX consoles, download device lists, merge them in Excel, compare against the asset inventory, identify gaps, and follow up on non-compliant devices.

Nobody asked me to automate this ‚Äî it wasn't in my job description. But I saw a process that was painful and inefficient, and I fixed it.
