I'll be honest ‚Äî in-house engineering roles, once processes stabilize, become repetitive. You're working the same tickets every day. Exclusion requests. Agent not reporting. Data ingestion issues. Someone needs a policy exception. The same patterns, different users.

Don't get me wrong ‚Äî I enjoyed building it. The transformation work was challenging. Designing the detection strategy, migrating from legacy tools, building the runbooks. But once the machine is running, you're maintaining it, not building anymore.

What I realized is that I enjoy the problem-solving at the start of projects ‚Äî assessing current state, identifying gaps, designing the approach, building the roadmap. That's the work that energizes me. In consulting, that's the core of what you do. New client, new environment, new challenges. You're always in that "figure it out" phase.

I'll learn faster seeing 10 different security programs than staying in one for 10 years.
Key message: Genuine reason ‚Äî enjoyed building, ready for variety
Avoid: Sounding negative about current role or saying "I'm bored"

INFLUENCE WITHOUT AUTHORITY

Q: Tell me about a time you had to influence without authority 90 sec

SITUATION When we rolled out Purview DLP, the Sales team pushed back hard. They saw it as IT policing their work. "You're going to block my deals" was the actual quote from a Sales Director.

TASK I needed to get their buy-in without any authority over them ‚Äî I couldn't force anything.

ACTION Instead of leading with security requirements, I asked to understand their workflow first. Spent time with a few reps, watched how they handle customer data, what tools they use.

Then I proposed a shadow mode pilot ‚Äî 30 days where we log but don't block. I'd share weekly reports showing what would have been blocked. This turned them from opponents to collaborators. They started flagging legitimate workflows we hadn't considered.

I also reframed the conversation: "This protects YOU. If customer data leaks from your laptop, it's your deal, your relationship, your reputation."

RESULT By the time we enabled blocking, they'd already adjusted their workflows. Zero escalations to leadership. The Sales Director who initially resisted became an advocate.
Why this works: Shows you can get buy-in from resistant stakeholders without escalating

CROSS-FUNCTIONAL COLLABORATION

Q: Tell me about a time you resolved a conflict between teams 60 sec

SITUATION Security wanted 90-day log retention in Sentinel for threat hunting. Legal wanted 7 years for compliance. Finance saw the cost projections and said no to both.

ACTION Instead of each team pushing their position, I mapped out the actual requirements. What data does Legal need for 7 years? It's not raw logs ‚Äî it's audit trails, access records, specific events for litigation holds.

I proposed tiered storage: hot storage for 90 days at full fidelity, then archive to cold storage with just the legally-required fields. Security gets threat hunting capability, Legal gets their retention, Finance gets 70% cost reduction versus keeping everything hot.

RESULT All three teams signed off. We implemented tiered retention with automated archival.
Why this works: Found a solution that met everyone's actual needs, not their stated positions

3Ô∏è‚É£ Challenges & Failures

BIGGEST FAILURE

Q: Tell me about your biggest failure 60-90 sec

SITUATION Three weeks into MDE rollout, our detection dashboard showed 347 devices with duplicate sensor IDs. Devices were overwriting each other's telemetry. We had major visibility gaps and didn't even know which devices were actually protected.

WHAT HAPPENED IT Ops had built golden images with MDE pre-installed and pre-onboarded. Every device cloned from that image inherited the same sensor ID. I should have caught this ‚Äî I reviewed their deployment docs but missed the golden image section.

ACTION First, containment. Identified affected device groups by deployment date. Coordinated with IT Ops on emergency re-onboarding ‚Äî offboard, delete sensor data, re-onboard with fresh IDs.

Then, fix the root cause. Updated the golden image process: install MDE but don't onboard. Onboarding happens at first boot via startup script. Added a detection rule that alerts on duplicate sensor IDs.

RESULT Took 5 days to fully remediate. But now it can't happen again. The monitoring rule has caught two near-misses since.
Key elements: Take ownership (didn't blame IT Ops), specific actions, systemic fix, lessons learned
Deep dive: MDE Deployment
DELIVERING BAD NEWS

Q: Tell me about a time you had to deliver bad news 60 sec

SITUATION During the SIEM migration, I discovered that 40% of our RSA NetWitness rules had never fired. Ever. Years of rules that looked good on paper but weren't detecting anything.

CHALLENGE The SOC manager had built many of these rules. This could feel like criticism of their work.

ACTION I framed it as a migration finding, not an audit. "Here's what I found when mapping rules to Sentinel. Good news: we can significantly reduce complexity." I showed the data ‚Äî detection coverage analysis, rules that duplicated XDR functionality, rules with no matching log sources.

Made it collaborative: "Which of these should we keep? Which do you want to redesign in Sentinel?" Gave them ownership of the decision.

RESULT We retired 780 rules. SOC manager appreciated the approach ‚Äî made their job easier, not harder.
Key elements: Data-driven, collaborative framing, preserved relationships

4Ô∏è‚É£ Consulting-Specific Scenarios

CLIENT DISAGREEMENT

Q: How would you handle a client who disagrees with your recommendation? 60 sec

First, I'd make sure I understand why they disagree. Is it technical concerns? Budget? Politics? Timeline? The surface objection often isn't the real issue.

If it's a legitimate difference of opinion, I'd present the tradeoffs clearly. "Option A has these benefits and these risks. Option B has these. Here's my recommendation and here's why, but here's what you'd need to mitigate if you go the other way."

Ultimately, it's their organization. My job is to give them the best advice and the information to make a good decision. Document the recommendation, document their decision, move forward as a partner.

The one exception: if it's a security decision that creates unacceptable risk ‚Äî like disabling MFA to make logins faster ‚Äî I'd escalate within our firm before proceeding.
Shows: Client-centric thinking, professional judgment, knowing when to escalate
CLIENT ETHICS

Q: A client asks you to do something you think is wrong. What do you do? 60 sec

Depends on what "wrong" means.

If it's unethical or illegal ‚Äî like falsifying audit evidence ‚Äî that's a hard no. I'd push back clearly, and if they insist, escalate immediately to my engagement manager and our risk team.

If it's technically wrong ‚Äî a bad architecture decision ‚Äî I'd explain the risks clearly, document my recommendation, but ultimately respect that it's their decision. They might have context I don't have.

If it's out of scope ‚Äî "Can you also fix our network while you're here?" ‚Äî I'd clarify scope boundaries and discuss whether this should be a change order.

The key is distinguishing between "I disagree" and "this is a red line."
UNKNOWN PROBLEM

Q: What if you realize you don't know how to solve the problem? 45 sec

Consulting is a team sport. First, I'd leverage the firm's network ‚Äî who's done this before? What knowledge bases exist? What past engagements are relevant?

I'd be transparent with the engagement manager: "Here's what I know, here's where I need support." Better to ask early than struggle silently.

With the client, I'd manage expectations without undermining confidence. "Let me research this and get back to you by end of day" is better than guessing.

In my experience, the ability to quickly learn and find answers is more valuable than already knowing everything. That's how I approached the security transformation ‚Äî I hadn't done a full Microsoft stack migration before, but I knew how to figure it out.

Q: Tell me about a time you went above and beyond 60 sec

RESULT Of 1,200+ legacy rules, 780 were redundant with XDR or never triggered. We built 485 Sentinel rules ‚Äî focused, high-value detections. Saved an estimated $20K per month in compute and storage, plus dramatically reduced alert fatigue for the SOC.

I wasn't asked to optimize ‚Äî just migrate. But migration without optimization would have been a missed opportunity.

CONFLICT

Q: Tell me about a time you disagreed with your manager 60 sec

During the SIEM migration planning, my manager wanted to do a direct lift-and-shift ‚Äî migrate every RSA rule to Sentinel, then optimize later. I disagreed. I thought we'd be recreating technical debt and the "later" optimization would never happen.

I didn't just push back with an opinion. I built a case: here's the rule overlap with XDR, here's the cost projection for migrating everything, here's the alternative approach with phased rationalization.

We had a good discussion. He was concerned about timeline risk ‚Äî what if rationalization took too long? So we compromised: we'd rationalize as we migrated, but set a hard deadline. If a rule couldn't be evaluated in time, we'd migrate it as-is and revisit later.

In the end, we rationalized 90% of rules during migration. The approach worked, and he acknowledged it was the right call.
Key elements: Respectful disagreement, data-driven argument, found compromise, positive outcome
PRIORITIZATION

Q: How do you prioritize when everything is urgent? 45 sec

I use a simple framework: impact vs. urgency, and I validate assumptions.

Not everything that feels urgent actually is. When multiple requests come in, I ask: What's the actual deadline? What's the business impact of delay? Who's waiting on this?

During the security transformation, I was managing SIEM migration, EDR rollout, and DLP implementation simultaneously. I couldn't do everything at once. So I mapped dependencies ‚Äî EDR had to be stable before we could retire legacy AV. SIEM detection coverage had to be validated before we could decommission RSA.

Then I communicated priorities clearly to stakeholders. "Here's what I'm focused on this sprint and why. Here's what's coming next." No surprises.
