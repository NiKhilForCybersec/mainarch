Q: How would you handle a client who disagrees with your recommendation? 60 sec

First, I'd listen to understand their concern‚Äîis it technical, budget, political, or something else? Often pushback isn't about the recommendation itself, it's about something we haven't surfaced.

If it's a genuine technical disagreement, I'd present data. "Here's why I recommended this approach‚Äîhere's the evidence, here's the risk if we go another direction." But I'd also acknowledge their expertise in their own environment‚Äîthey know things I don't.

If it's a budget or political constraint, I'd work with them to find an alternative that achieves the same outcome within their constraints. Maybe it's a phased approach, or a different technology.

What I wouldn't do is dig in just to be right. My job is to help them succeed, not to win arguments. If they have good reasons to go a different direction, I document the trade-offs and support their decision.
Key message: You're collaborative, not combative. Outcomes over ego.

Q: How would you approach a SOC maturity assessment for a new client? 90 sec

I'd structure it around four pillars: People, Process, Technology, and Governance.

People: Team structure, skills gaps, coverage model, turnover. Are they understaffed? Do they have the right expertise?

Process: Playbooks, incident response procedures, escalation paths. Are they documented? Are they followed? Do they improve over time?

Technology: SIEM, EDR, SOAR capabilities. What's deployed vs. what's actually used? Detection coverage against their threat model.

Governance: Metrics, SLAs, executive reporting. Do they know their MTTD and MTTR? Is security integrated into business decisions?

I'd score each pillar 1-5, identify gaps, and prioritize recommendations. The key insight is that your effective maturity is your lowest pillar‚Äîyou can have great tools but if process is weak, you're still Level 2.

The deliverable would include current state, target state, gap analysis, and a prioritized roadmap with quick wins and long-term initiatives.
Shows: Structured thinking, consulting methodology, practical application
Reference: Your SOC Maturity page has full details

Q: What's your view on SIEM vs. XDR? 60 sec

It's not either/or‚Äîit's about playing to each platform's strengths.

XDR excels at what it can see: endpoints, identity, email, cloud apps. It has kernel-level visibility, behavioral ML, and vendor-maintained detection content. For endpoint threats, XDR will always be better than SIEM queries against logs.

SIEM adds value where XDR stops: cross-domain correlation, business context, third-party data sources, compliance logging. "Terminated employee accessing sensitive data" needs HR context that XDR doesn't have.

My approach is XDR-first: let XDR handle what it does best, use SIEM to fill gaps. This reduces SIEM cost, alert fatigue, and gives you better detection coverage than trying to do everything in SIEM.

Q: How do you measure security program effectiveness? 60 sec

I focus on outcomes, not activity metrics.

Detection effectiveness: MTTD‚Äîare we finding threats faster over time? Detection coverage‚Äîwhat percentage of relevant ATT&CK techniques can we detect?

Response effectiveness: MTTR‚Äîare we containing and resolving faster? SLA compliance‚Äîare we meeting our commitments?

Operational health: False positive rate‚Äîare we wasting analyst time? Alert-to-incident ratio‚Äîare our detections producing actionable results?

Business metrics: Cost per incident, risk reduction, compliance posture.

The key is trending, not point-in-time. Are we improving? And are we measuring things that matter for the client's specific risk profile?

Q: What security trends are you watching? 45 sec

A few things I'm paying attention to:

Identity as the new perimeter: With remote work and cloud, identity is where attacks start. MFA bypass techniques, session token theft‚Äîthese are the attacks that matter now.

AI in security: Both offensive and defensive. Attackers using AI for phishing at scale, defenders using it for anomaly detection and automation. The winners will be those who adopt intelligently.

XDR consolidation: Organizations moving from best-of-breed to integrated platforms. Trade-offs between specialization and operational simplicity.

OT/IT convergence: Manufacturing and critical infrastructure increasingly connected. IT security teams now responsible for OT environments they don't fully understand.

Q: When would you recommend RSA NetWitness vs Splunk vs Sentinel? 90 sec

It depends on the client's environment, maturity, and priorities. Here's how I think about it:

RSA NetWitness ‚Äî I'd recommend this for highly regulated environments that need deep forensic visibility. Financial services, government, critical infrastructure. The strength is full packet capture and session reconstruction ‚Äî you can see exactly what happened at the network level. But it's heavy infrastructure, expensive, and not cloud-native. Best for organizations with mature SOCs and on-prem requirements.

Splunk ‚Äî When the client needs maximum flexibility. Splunk works with almost any data source, has a powerful query language, and strong ecosystem. Good for large enterprises with complex, hybrid environments and diverse log sources. The trade-off is cost at scale and operational overhead ‚Äî you need skilled engineers to get value from it.

Microsoft Sentinel ‚Äî For cloud-first, Microsoft-heavy environments. Native integration with MDE, MDI, MDO, Entra ID ‚Äî you get faster time-to-value and lower operational overhead. Built-in UEBA, auto-scaling, consumption-based pricing. The trade-off is less raw packet visibility and you're committing to the Microsoft ecosystem.

The real answer is: understand the client's environment first. What's their infrastructure? Cloud or on-prem? What's their team's skill level? What are their compliance requirements? Then recommend based on fit, not personal preference.
Structure: Framework (it depends) ‚Üí Each option with strengths/trade-offs ‚Üí Real answer is "understand the client first"
Shows: Platform-agnostic thinking, consulting mindset, client-first approach

Q: A client has a legacy SIEM and wants to modernize. How would you approach the assessment? 90 sec

I'd structure the assessment in three phases: Discovery, Analysis, and Roadmap.

Discovery: First, understand what they have. Export their current rules, log sources, integrations. Interview the SOC team to understand what actually gets worked versus what generates noise. Review incident data to see what detections actually caught real threats.

Analysis: Map their detections to outcomes, not just rules. "What attacks are you trying to detect?" Then evaluate coverage gaps‚Äîtypically I find legacy SIEMs over-detect on network and under-detect on identity and cloud. Also assess operational maturity: do they have playbooks, automation, metrics?

Roadmap: Build a phased migration plan tied to business priorities. What can stay, what needs to migrate, what can retire because modern XDR handles it natively? Include parallel operation period so there's no gap in coverage.

The key insight is: don't migrate rules 1-to-1. Migrate outcomes. Often you end up with better coverage with fewer, higher-fidelity detections.
Structure: Discovery ‚Üí Analysis ‚Üí Roadmap
Consulting mindset: "Migrate outcomes, not rules"
Shows: You've done this before and have a methodology

Q: How do you decide what goes in SIEM vs. XDR? 60 sec

I think about it in terms of detection ownership.

XDR owns: Endpoint behaviors, identity attacks, email threats‚Äîanything where the native product has deeper visibility than SIEM. XDR has kernel-level hooks, AMSI integration, behavioral ML that you can't replicate with log queries.

SIEM owns: Cross-domain correlation, non-Microsoft sources, business logic, and compliance. When you need to join identity events with network logs with application data‚Äîthat's SIEM territory.

The anti-pattern I see is duplicating XDR detections in SIEM "just in case." That creates noise, wastes ingestion cost, and means the SOC sees the same alert twice. Better approach: trust XDR for its domain, use SIEM for correlation, and have the two feed into a unified incident queue.
Framework: Detection ownership ‚Äî who has better visibility?
Anti-pattern: Duplicating XDR detections in SIEM
Shows: You understand modern XDR-first architecture

Q: A client wants to ingest all their logs into SIEM. What would you advise? 60 sec

I'd push back‚Äîrespectfully. "Ingest everything" is how you end up with a million-dollar SIEM bill and a SOC that can't find anything.

My approach is detection-driven ingestion. Start with: "What are you trying to detect?" Then: "What logs do those detections require?" Only ingest what you'll actually use.

For the remaining logs, I'd propose a tiered strategy. High-value logs go to analytics tier for full querying. Verbose logs go to basic or archive tier for compliance and forensics. And some logs can stay in their native systems‚Äîyou don't need to centralize everything if you can query it when needed.

The business case is usually compelling: clients who take a detection-driven approach often cut ingestion costs by 50-70% while improving detection coverage because they're focused on signal, not noise.
Approach: Detection-driven ingestion, not "ingest everything"
Tiered strategy: Analytics / Basic / Archive / Stay in place
Business value: "50-70% cost reduction with better coverage"

Q: What's your approach to threat detection use case development? 75 sec

I follow a structured methodology.

Start with threats, not logs: "What attacks are we worried about?" Use MITRE ATT&CK as a framework‚Äîidentify techniques relevant to the client's environment and threat model.

Map to data sources: For each technique, what telemetry would show it? This drives log ingestion decisions‚Äîyou don't ingest data that doesn't support a use case.

Design with fidelity in mind: A good detection is specific enough to catch real attacks but not so broad it drowns the SOC in false positives. I look at attacker behavior patterns, not just individual events.

Test and tune: Deploy in audit mode, validate against baseline, tune thresholds, then move to alerting. Build in feedback loop with SOC so detections improve over time.

The key is: detections should be actionable. If the SOC doesn't know what to do when it fires, it's not a detection‚Äîit's noise.
Methodology: Threats ‚Üí Data sources ‚Üí Detection logic ‚Üí Test/tune
Framework: MITRE ATT&CK for coverage mapping

Q: What do you know about API Security? 60 sec

Application Security is about shifting security left‚Äîfinding and fixing vulnerabilities before they reach production.
