5. How do you manage SIEM ingestion cost?

Very common for Sentinel roles. Show you think about money.

Say This

"The number one optimization is detection-driven ingestion ‚Äî design first, ingest second. We delayed full Sentinel ingestion until the detection catalog was defined. XDR works independently, so we rolled out MDE, got detections from Day 1, while keeping Sentinel ingestion minimal until we knew exactly what logs each detection needed.

The second big win is XDR-first selective ingestion. With XDR enabled, we consume XDR incidents, not all raw Device tables. XDR already correlates that telemetry into high-fidelity incidents. Before optimization: 150 GB/day in endpoint data. After: 25 GB/day. That's 80% reduction.

We also use commitment tiers ‚Äî 100 GB/day tier gives roughly 50% discount. But we moved to commitment tier only after steady-state volume was known, not Day 1 when it was unpredictable.

Result: Sentinel costs about $12K/month. Legacy RSA was $35K. That's 66% savings with better coverage."

7. How do you decide between built-in detections vs custom rules?

Great consultant question. Efficiency and maintainability.

Say This

"My default is built-in first, custom only when necessary.

Built-in when Microsoft provides a native equivalent, the logic is well-maintained, and you want automatic updates when new threats emerge. I enabled Microsoft's AiTM phishing detection rather than writing custom KQL ‚Äî they have more threat intel and update it constantly.

Custom when it's business-specific logic, environment-specific thresholds, or no built-in exists. We wrote custom for 'service principal granted privileged role outside change window' ‚Äî that requires knowing our change windows.

Every custom rule is technical debt ‚Äî you maintain it forever. In our migration, about 70% came from built-in rules enabled and tuned, 30% custom for genuine gaps."

Block 4: Operations

8. How do you reduce alert fatigue?

Very common question. Show operational thinking.

Say This

"Alert fatigue is one of the biggest operational problems. My approach:

First, tune before you enable. Never enable a detection at vendor defaults. Run in audit mode first, analyze the FP rate, then adjust thresholds before going live. We reduced initial alert volume by 75% through pre-deployment tuning.

Second, environment-specific exclusions. Whitelist known-good behavior ‚Äî service accounts that legitimately do suspicious things, scheduled tasks that look like persistence but aren't.

Third, correlation and grouping. Combine related low-confidence signals into higher-confidence incidents. Ten alerts, one incident to triage.

And finally, retire bad detections. If a rule has less than 10% true positive rate after tuning, disable it. A rule generating 100 false positives and 1 true positive is worse than no rule. Our SOC went from 800 alerts per day to about 150 through systematic tuning."

  • "Tune before you enable"
  • "75% reduction through pre-deployment tuning"
  • "Environment-specific exclusions"
  • "Ten alerts, one incident"
  • "800 to 150 alerts/day"

1. TUNE BEFORE ENABLE

Block 5: Consulting Mindset

9. How do you handle conflicts between business requirements and security?

Classic consultant scenario. Risk trade-offs, not "security says no."

Say This

"My approach is risk-based trade-offs, not 'security says no.'

First, understand the business need. Why do they need this? What outcome are they trying to achieve? Often there's a secure way to get the same result.

Then quantify the risk. Don't say 'that's insecure.' Say 'if we do this, here's the specific risk ‚Äî unauthorized access to customer data ‚Äî and the likelihood based on these factors.'

Offer alternatives. 'I understand you need to share files externally. Instead of disabling DLP, let's create an approved channel with logging. You get functionality, we keep visibility.'

Real example: An app team wanted to exclude their entire server from MDE because of performance issues. Instead of saying no, I worked with them to find the three specific processes causing overhead. They got their performance, we kept detection coverage for everything else.

Security's job isn't to say no ‚Äî it's to help the business achieve goals securely."
